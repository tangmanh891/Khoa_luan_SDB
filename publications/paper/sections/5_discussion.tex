\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The controlled ablation clarifies where the measured gains come from: Focal Loss and
many-hot supervision do not produce consistent gains over the BCE control,
whereas calibrated post-processing---Gaussian smoothing to change boundary
decisions and temperature scaling to improve probability quality---provides
the measured improvement. Because post-processing operates on cached logits,
it can be re-tuned for a new deployment domain without retraining the
representation. The fixed-deployment ClipShots result is limited primarily by
aggregate precision (\PaperDeployClipPrecision): open-world content produces
false-positive peaks that survive smoothing. A frozen representation cannot
adapt to this distribution shift by construction, so closing the gap likely
requires domain-adaptive components rather than further tuning of the present
loss-design space.

After temperature scaling, the equal-width ECE falls to 0.002214, yet the
class-balanced ECE remains 0.070405. Boundary sparsity explains the gap:
non-boundary frames dominate aggregate bins, so a low aggregate score can
coexist with poorer positive-class calibration. Reporting only aggregate ECE
would overstate calibration quality for SBD and likely for other sparse
temporal-event tasks.

The two uncertainty analyses answer complementary questions. Paired bootstrap
intervals estimate how much measured deltas depend on which videos are
sampled; the three-seed study estimates sensitivity to head-training
randomness. Seed-induced F1 standard deviations are below 0.5 percentage
points on every dataset and much smaller than the bootstrap interval widths,
indicating that video sampling is the larger source of variation.
