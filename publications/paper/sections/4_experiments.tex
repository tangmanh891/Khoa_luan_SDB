\subsection{Evaluation Tracks and Reproducibility Tiers}
\label{sec:tracks}

The paper separates deployment evaluation from controlled component analysis.
\emph{Track 1 (deployed checkpoint)} provides the fixed-threshold deployment results: the head
trained for deployment with the fixed configuration
$T^{*}=\PaperDeployTemperature$, $\sigma=\PaperGaussianSigma$, and
$\theta=\PaperDeployThreshold$. We regenerated its test logits from the local
video splits with full coverage (200/200 SHOT, 11/11 BBC, and 500/500
ClipShots videos) and checksum the resulting logits and result JSON files.
\emph{Track 2 (controlled replication)} retrains the head under the frozen
validation-only protocol of Section~\ref{sec:protocol} (configuration B4) with
full logit caches. Paired bootstrap intervals, calibration diagnostics,
ablations, and the seed study are anchored on this controlled track. Because
the replication was trained in a separate environment with its own validation
pool, its absolute scores differ slightly from the deployed checkpoint. To
avoid mixing absolute scales, replication analyses are reported as
\emph{differences} against the internal BCE control (A1), while absolute F1
values for deployment in the paper refer to the deployed checkpoint.

\subsection{Main Results Under Per-Dataset Operating-Point Selection}
\label{subsec:main_results}

Table~\ref{tab:overall} reports the main comparison under per-dataset operating-point selection. This protocol selects the best decision threshold for each evaluation dataset and is intended to measure the attainable dataset-specific performance of the frozen-feature refinement pipeline. Because this protocol is not a single-threshold deployment setting, we report the fixed deployment results separately in Section~\ref{subsec:fixed_deploy}.

Under this per-dataset selection protocol, AutoShotV2 reaches peak F1 scores of 0.8607 on SHOT, 0.9656 on BBC, and 0.7706 on ClipShots. These outcomes outperform the reproduced AutoShot baseline (0.8405 on SHOT, 0.9554 on BBC, and 0.7649 on ClipShots) by 2.02, 1.02, and 0.57 percentage points, respectively. On BBC, this configuration outperforms the reproduced TransNetV2 baseline (0.9593) by 0.63 percentage points.

On ClipShots, the peak F1 score of 0.7706 (Table~\ref{tab:extra_sweep_peaks}) also outperforms the reported literature scores of DSM (0.7030) by 6.76 percentage points and TransNet (V1) (0.7350) by 3.56 percentage points, and exceeds the reproduced TransNetV2 baseline (0.7454) by 2.52 percentage points. To distinguish protocol sources, baseline labels indicate whether they are cited from literature or evaluated within our pipeline. Specifically, reproduced baselines are evaluated using our pipeline, whereas reported baselines (for DSM, TransNet, and TransNetV2 on SHOT) are cited from their original publications. Note that the TransNetV2 model was not evaluated on SHOT in the original work; the reported SHOT score of 0.7993 is cited from \cite{zhu2023autoshot}.

\subsection{Fixed-Threshold Deployment Analysis}
\label{subsec:fixed_deploy}

To assess deployment robustness under real-world conditions where domain-specific threshold tuning may not be feasible, we evaluate AutoShotV2 under a single, unified decision threshold ($\theta = \PaperDeployThreshold$). Under this fixed deployment setting, AutoShotV2 achieves F1 scores of 0.8545 on SHOT, 0.9656 on BBC, and 0.7529 on ClipShots. 

While the fixed deployment threshold maintains strong robustness and competitive performance on SHOT and BBC, it reveals a notable precision bottleneck on open-world video footage. Specifically, on ClipShots, the dataset-specific operating point reaches 0.7706, exceeding the reproduced AutoShot baseline by 0.57 percentage points. However, under the single fixed deployment threshold, the score drops to 0.7529, indicating that the main remaining challenge is domain-specific operating-point selection and false-positive control under open-world effects.

\subsection{Supplemental Configuration and Oracle Analysis}
\label{sec:oracle_sweep}

In addition to the primary AutoShotV2 configuration, we conduct an oracle analysis using a supplemental configuration with a revised training and post-processing setup. Specifically, we set $T^* = 0.2413$, $\sigma = 2.0$, Focal Loss parameters $\gamma = 2.0$ and $\alpha = 0.6$, many-hot supervision weight to $0.3$, learning rate to $7 \times 10^{-6}$, and weight decay to $10^{-4}$. The goal is to investigate whether increasing the focusing parameter of Focal Loss ($\gamma = 2.0$ vs. $1.0$) and reducing the learning rate can yield higher peak F1 scores when the decision threshold is optimized independently for each target dataset (oracle thresholding).

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:extra_sweep_peaks}, this supplemental configuration achieves superior peak F1 scores on SHOT (0.8607 vs. 0.8545, a gain of 0.62 percentage points) and ClipShots (0.7706 vs. 0.7529, a gain of 1.77 percentage points) when decision thresholds are optimized individually for each dataset.

Specifically, the "per-dataset oracle best" row in Table~\ref{tab:overall} consolidates these peak outcomes by selecting the best F1 score per dataset across both the primary and supplemental configurations. This yields 0.8607 on SHOT (from the supplemental configuration), 0.7706 on ClipShots (from the supplemental configuration), and 0.9656 on BBC (from the primary deployment configuration, which outperforms the 0.9648 achieved by the supplemental configuration).

However, this supplemental setup is less robust than the primary configuration under a unified deployment threshold. For example, the best joint threshold for this supplemental configuration ($\theta = 0.17$) yields F1 scores of 0.8518 on SHOT, 0.7581 on ClipShots, and 0.9598 on BBC, resulting in an average F1 of 0.8566. This joint performance is lower than the primary deployment average of 0.8577 across the three datasets. Consequently, while these results reveal domain-specific optimization potential, we retain the primary configuration for the fixed-threshold deployment analysis.

\subsection{Training-Seed Robustness}
\label{sec:seed_robustness}

To quantify optimization variability, we retrained the BCE one-hot head
\PaperSeedCount{} times with training seeds 42, 43, and 44 while holding the
data seed, the training-video selection, and the sample cache identity fixed.
Each run selected its own post-processing configuration with the frozen
five-fold validation-only protocol of Section~\ref{sec:protocol} before any
test evaluation. Consistent with the delta-only reporting of
Section~\ref{sec:tracks}, we summarize this study by its dispersion: the
sample standard deviation of test F1 across seeds is
\PaperSeedShotFOneStd{} percentage points on SHOT,
\PaperSeedBBCFOneStd{} percentage points on BBC, and
\PaperSeedClipFOneStd{} percentage points on ClipShots --- below
0.5 percentage points on every dataset and an
order of magnitude smaller than the paired bootstrap interval widths of
Table~\ref{tab:paired_delta}. In these runs, head-training randomness is a
smaller source of variation than video sampling. This study isolates
training-seed sensitivity end to end, complementing the component analysis of
Table~\ref{tab:ablation}.

\subsection{Limitations}

The study has five important limitations. First, although the deployment
checkpoint logits have been regenerated from complete local video splits, the
three-seed study of Section~\ref{sec:seed_robustness} measures optimization
variability for the controlled pipeline and does not reproduce either
checkpoint bit for bit. Second, the historical sample cache recorded 400
videos and 33,174 frames but not the selected IDs; the current manifests fix
this for future runs but cannot reconstruct the old cache contents. Third,
validation calibration diagnostics reuse the temperature-selection data.
Fourth, the frozen representation does not fully control cross-domain false
positives on ClipShots. An exploratory experiment that partially unfroze the
layer nearest to the classification head reached a ClipShots F1 of 0.7962
but degraded BBC to 0.9341, confirming a multi-domain trade-off that the
fully-frozen regime avoids at the cost of lower ClipShots precision. This
trade-off is not analyzed further in the present paper, which focuses
exclusively on the frozen-feature setting.
Fifth, the main comparison under per-dataset operating-point selection represents an optimistic upper bound (domain-specific threshold tuning) rather than a single unified deployment setting. In practice, finding domain-specific thresholds requires labeled test data from the target distribution, meaning that the peak F1 scores of 0.8607 on SHOT and 0.7706 on ClipShots should be interpreted as potential estimates of frozen-feature capability rather than out-of-the-box deployment results.
